Angular Kinematics

You don't have to be as smart as Einstein to know that when you ride your bicycle around a curve, you must lean or tilt the bike toward the center of the curve. As a biomechanist, you should know why you have to lean toward the center of the curve,

 

As the bicycle travells along the curve, friction keeps the tires from sliding off the curve. The friction force is equal but opposite to the mass times the radial acceleration which is the centrifugal force tending to push the cyclist off the curve. This friction force is called "centripetal force". The two forces (centrifugal and centripetal) are equal but in opposite directions which keeps the cyclist on the curved path. However, the centrifugal force acts at the center of gravity of the cyclist and the centripetal (friction) force acts at the tires. This creates a moment about the tires tending to topple the cyclist over toward the outside of the curve. To make the moment equal to zero, The cyclist leans toward the center of the curve so that the gravitational force creates a moment that is equal but opposite to the moment cause by the centrifugal force.

 

Example: Find theta (the amount of lean) required to keep a cyclist on a curve of 20 m radius if the cyclist's velocity is 10 m/s. The center of gravity of the cyclist is 1.1 m above the ground.

Solution: Find the centrifugal force,  The centrifugal force is equal to the cyclist's mass and the radial acceleration. The radial acceleration is equal to the radius of the curve multiplied by the square of the angular velocity. The angular velocity is equal to the tangetial velocity divided by the radius.

In this case, the tangential velocity is 10 m/s and the radius is 20 m. Therefore, the angular velocity is 10/20 = 0.5 rad/s. The radial acceleration is 20(0.5)2 = 5.0 m/s2.

Drawing the FBD of the cyclist, we see that N and F will not cause a moment about the axis and the negative moment caused by the gravitational force must be equal to the positive moment caused by the centrifugal force. We know that the centrigual force is equal to m(5.0) and the gravitational force in m(9.81). The moment arm of the centrifugal force is 1.1 sin theta while the moment of the gravitational force is 1.1 cos theta.

Therefore, for static equilibrium, m(5.0)(1.1)sin theta = m(9.91)(1.1) cos theta.

Solve for theta when the sum of the moment about the tires is zero.

The faster the bike travels, the greater the lean and the greater the amount of required friction.
