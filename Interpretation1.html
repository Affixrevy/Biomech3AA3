Interpretation of Net Joint Moments

In the preceding lecture we calculated joint reaction forces and net joint moments about the ankle, knee and hip for a single frame of a walking stride.  In order to complete the analysis, this procedure would need to be repeated for each frame of the complete stride. In practice, biomechanists use computer software to perform these repeated calculations and plot the net joint moments versus time.  The figure below shows the results of these calculations for three repeated trials of a hip replacement patient.  The data were calculated for the stance phase of walking from heel contact (HC) to toe off (TO).

The bottom graph shows the ankle moment and we can see that the plantar flexors are the dominant group for almost the entire stance phase. 

The middle graph shows the knee moment which is dominated by the knee extensors.

The top graph shows the hip moments which are dominated by the extensors early in stance and then the flexors in late stance.

We can see that there is some variability in the three strides and we will discuss a method to deal with this variabilty at the bottom of this page.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Limitations:

1) The net joint moment does not yield any information on antagonist co-contraction.  In the graph above, a positive 20 N.m knee moment indicates that the knee extensors are the dominant group but it is possible that the knee extensors are actually producing 25 N.m and the knee flexors are co-contracting with 5 N.m of flexor moment for a net 20 N.m extensor moment.

2) The net joint moment does not distinguish between active muscle moments and passive moments caused by ligaments or joint constraints. Even muscle can generate force passively.  If we take a relaxed subject and move the joint through the range of motion, we can feel the passive resistance of the stiffness and viscosity of the tissues crossing the joint and the dramatic resistance of the joint constraints when we get to the extreme range of motion.

Considerations:

1) Equipment needed for data collection

    - video or optoelectronics (2D or 3D), goniometers, accelerometers

    - external forces (force platforms, strain gauges)

2) Data processing

    - A/D conversion, digitizing, calibration

    - filtering, differentiation, integration

3) Accuracy and validity

    - assumptions of rigid link model

        - body segments interconnected hinge joints (2D) and ball-socket joints (3D)

        - length of each segment is fixed

        - each segment has a constant mass with a fixed center of mass location

        - moment of inertia is fixed about the center of mass

        - net joint moment indicates the dominant muscle group

4) Generalization - As mentioned above, there seems to be variability in the the way people perform repeated tasks.  We need a method to obtain the charateristic pattern of their performance.  The most widely used method is the "Ensemble Average".

Ensemble Average

The proceedure of obtaining the ensemble average requires a normalization of repeated in trials in the time domain.  The data are interpolated to have the same number of data points or frames from one event to the other.  For instance, it is a common practice to normalize the walking stride to have 60 points during the stance phase and 40 points during the swing and plot the data as a percent of the entire stride.  This prevents different speeds of moving from adding to the variabilty of the movement.

The next step is to calculate the mean and standard deviation of each data point (percentage) of the movement.  The figures below show the ensemble average of a single subject with 10 repeated trials (left) and the ensemble average of 19 healthy subjects (right).

CV refers to the coefficient of variation which is the variance divided by the mean and allows comparisons to be made between different joints and different subjects.  We can see on the left that the CV is quite small for the ankle but a bit larger for the knee and the hip.  We often compensate at one joint for variation in another joint.  The support moment is simply a sum of the hip, knee, and ankle moments and we can see that the CV is only 14% which is lower than any individual joint.  Therefore, if a subject exerts less at the knee and compensates with more at the hip, the sum is the same.

The variance of the population of 19 subjects (right) is quite a bit higher than repeated trials of a single subject (left).  All subjects were healthy but had different ways of walking.  When the population data base has such large variation, it makes a diagnosis more difficult.
