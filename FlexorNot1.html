When is a Flexor Not a Flexor?

During the rowing cycle, rowers sit on a sliding seat with their feet held stationary.  The Gastrocnemius muscle is generally considered to be a two-joint muscle that tends to plantar flex the ankle and flex the knee joint. In 1903, Lombard postulated that under certain conditions, a two-joint muscle might have the opposite effect and he used the term "pseudo antagonist".  For a more complete discussion on this topic please feel free to read Art Kuo's paper on Lombard's Paradox.

In order to keep the rower in static equilibrium, there must be a 24.7 N force preventing the seat from sliding to the right.  In reality, the rollers on the seat would prevent static equilibrium, the leg and thigh segments would accelerate and an exact calculation of the vertical and horizontal reaction forces would need to include the inertial component of force.  However, the reaction forces would only change in magnitude and the motion would be an extension of the knee.  Therefore, since the Gastrocnemius muscle is the only force acting (other than reaction forces), the result is an extension moment about the knee joint and a motion that slides the seat to the right.  In this situation, the Gastrocnemius muscle contributes to knee extension.  Furthermore, if we were to calculate the length change of the Gastrocnemius, we would see that the muscle actually shortens from this point to full knee extension which indicates positive power in a concentric contraction. It also needs to be stated that when the knee is flexed and the hips are quite close to the feet, the Gastrocnemius would be a flexor of the knee - there is a critical angle for theta in the above example when the muscle changes from a flexor moment to an extensor moment.

When we discussed joint power and muscle power in the "Power Flow" lecture, we would calculate the knee moment to be an extensor moment and assume that the knee extensors were the dominant group. We would then multiply the net extensor moment by the angular velocity of the thigh (which would be a positive power) and assume that the knee extensor muscles were generating power and doing positive work on the thigh segment. There would also be a generation of power by the knee extensors to the leg segment. By integrating power to get the work done, we would get the correct value of muscle work. However, we would attribute all of the work to the knee extensor muscle group when in reality, some of that work was done by Gastrocnemius.

Consider the two joint, six muscle system pictured to the right.  Four of the muscles are simple one-joint muscles that flex or extend the two joints.  Muscles 5 and 6 are two-joint muscles.

Muscle 6 would anatomically be defined as a flexor of joint 1 and as an extensor of joint 2.  However, we can see that due to the vertical sliding constraint, if muscle 6 is active, it will cause segment 2 to slide upward which will extend joint 2 and also joint 1 even though it is a flexor of joint 1.

Much like our example of the rower's Gastrocnemius, in a power analysis, we would calculate the correct amount of work done but we would attribute some of the work done by muscle 6 to the extensors of joint 1. Muscle 6 would still be considered to have contributed to the work done in extending joint 2 but not it's contribution to the work at joint 1.

If we consider muscle 6 to be contracting isometrically (neither stretching or shortening), then we can see as muscle 1 extends joint 1 muscle 6 will extend joint 2 by virtue of the fact that it will not stretch or lengthen even though joint 1 is extending. This isometric contraction is not doing any work but it is transferring from the work done by muscle 1 to the top segment. Again, in a power analysis, we would calculate an extensor moment about joint 2 and assume that work was done by the extensors of joint 2 when really, all of the work was done by muscle 1 (an extensor of joint 2) with some of that work passively transferred to the top segment by muscle 6.

Suppose we were to simplify the two segment system to have only muscle 1. We can see that if muscle 1 contracts, it will cause the lower segment to extend but joint 2 will also extend by sliding upward due to the constraint of the sliding mechanism.

Does this mean that the one joint muscle crossing joint 1 can be considered an extensor of joint 2?

The answer is no. If we did a linked segment analysis, we would find a net joint moment at joint 1 that would indicate an extensor dominant group. The net joint moment at joint 2 would be zero but there would be joint reaction forces at joint 2. The power analysis would reveal the work done by muscle 1 by the muscle power at joint 1 and there would be passive joint power at joint 2 showing some of that work increasing the energy of the top segment.

The joint power at the top would be zero because the velocity in the horizontal direction is zero, and the reaction force in the vertical direction is zero. Since power is force times velocity, the horizontal reaction force is multiplied by a zero velocity and the vertical velocity is multiplied by a zero force.

Summary:

It may be convenient to classify muscle actions according to apparent moment arms due to origin and insertion.  However, the actual consequences of activating muscles under various conditions can be contrary to those classifications. A full biomechanical analysis that includes estimations of individual muscle forces is required to determine the exact role of a muscle in terms of actual consequences and measures of actual work.  In that sense, there really is no paradox only an incomplete understanding of the potential roles that a muscle can have.

Briefly, a muscle can be considered to have an opposite action at a joint if each of the following conditions applies

- the muscle crosses more than a single joint;

- the motion is constrained to move to a certain direction;

- the moment due to the constraint reaction is greater and opposite to the direct moment of the muscle about that joint.
