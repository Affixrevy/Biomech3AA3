Carpal Tunnel Syndrome:

A nerve disorder in the hand that causes pain and loss of feeling, especially in the thumb and the first fingers.

This disorder occurs when the median nerve, traveling through the "tunnel" of the wrist bones, is compressed by the tendons which also run through the carpal tunnel (see the figure below).

 

 

 

 

 

It can be seen that the median nerve must compete for space with the tendons that attach to the extrinsic muscles that control the fingers.There is considerable movement of the tendons which further affects the median nerve. There is a higher incidence of this disorder with women and one thought for the difference is that a smaller hand has less space for the median nerve.

 

 

Detection:

Biomechanics has been used in the diagnosis and detection because nerve entrapment is often associated with slowing of conduction velocity along the nerve.  Electrodes are placed above the nerve and the action potential is measured at with two pairs of electrodes at a known distance apart (see figure below). Causes of CTS:

- arthritis, diabetes

- fractures

- repetitive, prolonged activity

- excessive force (tunnel shape friction)

- posture (wrist extension can cause CTS)

- palmer compression

- pinching is worse than gripping

- tools that vibrate

- cold working environment

Prevention:

- gloves that decrease friction and decrease the required grip force and that reduce vibration amplitude and increase temperature.

- reduce repetition (job rotation)

- tool design (avoid posture of wrist extension)
