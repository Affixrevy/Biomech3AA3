Lab 1: The Scientific Method

The goal of science is to explain.  Before you can explain, you must be able to describe.  Before you can describe, you must be able to measure.  This lab leads into issues concerning biomechanical measurements but first, we will look at the scientific method and understand how scientists think, discover, prove and explain.

Ways of Knowing:

    1) method of authority - an expert told me it is true so it must be true.

    2) method of tenacity - I have always known it to be true and I'm not changing.

    3) a priori method - it seems self evident, agrees with reason or my intuition tells me it is true.

    4) method of science - Unlike the methods above, this method has the ability of self correction.  This method systematically builds theories, subjects them to rigorous testing, realizes that they may not reflect reality even if they fit all present observations and never accepts the metaphysical as an explanation.  A more complete description is given below.

Method of Science:

It is that method for describing and explaining phenomena which incorporates the principles of empirical verification, operational definition, controlled observation, statistical generalization, and empirical confirmation.

    1) Empirical Verification - A descriptive statement is regarded as true if and only if it is found to correspond with observed reality.

- empirical means based on experiment

- observations by others can be accepted if reported properly (I can replicate their experiment)

    2) Operational Definition - All terms in a descriptive statement must be carefully defined in terms of operations involved in manipulating or observing their referents.

- confines our statements to things we can define

- forces us to talk in a way that communicates our exact meaning to others (no jargon, slang or ambiguity)

    3) Controlled Observation - For an observation to provide an adequate test of the statement that a change on A causes a change on B, variable B must be observed when different values on variable A occur and, moreover, when all other variables can be discounted as possible causes of any change observed on B.

- experimental control (only variable A changes)

- statistical control (other variables change but their effect can be calculated and subtracted)

- assumed invariance (other variables are assumed to not change)

- assumed irrelevance (effects of other variables are assumed to not have any effect on A)

- randomization (control by chance)

    4) Statistical Generalization - observations can be generalized to conditions other than those under which it was made if the conditions constitute an adequate random sample of the set of conditions to which you want to generalize.

- statistical power

    5) Empirical confirmation - An explanatory statement is regarded as being more probably true as the number of verified descriptive statements increases.

- hypothesis is an untested descriptive statement

-experiment attempts to reject the null hypothesis (no effect)

- rejection of the null hypothesis does not mean an acceptance of the hypothesis (significance levels)

- many alternative hypotheses need to be tested

- a theory needs to explain all observable phenomena to be considered a law.

Concluding Remarks:

Not all experiments are equally valid.  Our ability to explain is based on the strength of our arguments and our rigor in applying the scientific method.  Some questions lend themselves better to this method than others but it is important to look critically at how well we are applying the scientific method and how wrong our conclusions might be.  Instead of looking for evidence that supports our beliefs, it is more important to search for evidence that contradicts our present understanding.  It is through the systematic elimination of null hypotheses that allows us to progress toward an explained universe.
