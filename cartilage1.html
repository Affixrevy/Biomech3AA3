Articular Cartilage

Articular cartilage is the dense, white connective tissue that covers the ends of each bone in an articulating joint. The purpose of articular cartilage is to protect ends of bones by distributing the load over a greater area and to reduce friction within the joint. The diagram at the right shows an articulating joint that has a joint capsule filled with synovial fluid. 

Composition of Articular Cartilage:

- 60-85% water.

- 10-39% collagen

-3-10% proteoglycan gel

- 1-2% chondrocytes.

Mechanical Properties:

Collagen fibers are arrnged in criss-crossed matrix. Proteoglycan gels create a swelling pressure that puts the collagen fibers in tension.  As the cartilage is loaded in compression, the collagen fibers are still in tension (much like the latex in a ballon).  The membrane is semi-permiable and allows water to move across the membrane but does not allow the proteoglycan gels to leave the cartilage.  As water is squeezed out of the cartilage, in concentration of proteoglycan gels increases which increases the swelling pressure.  This mechanism helps protect the cartilage from being compressed too much.

The diagram to the right shows the response of articular cartilage to a constant load.  At first, water leaves the cartilage rapidly which causes a rapid deformation.  As the concentration of the proteoglycan gels increases, less water leaves.  This gradual deformation is called "creep" and is not like a typical spring of constant stiffness which deforms instantaneously.

Standing for long periods of time results in a large creep deformation of the cartilage which reduces its ability to protect the bones and causes joint pain.  For this reason, we shift our weight from one foot to the other to allow the water to flow back into the cartilage.

Lubrication:

As the joint rotates, the cartilage exudes water in front of and under the stress area which improves lubrication and decreases wear (see below). After peak load, fluid is re-absorbed back into the articular cartilage.

Wear:

Wear is defined as the removal of material via mechanical action. Wear results in reduced collagen fibers which reduces the strength of the cartilage and the stiffness of the matrix.

Arthritis:

Arthritis is a condition that makes the joints swell. This swelling is the result of bones rubbing against each other without the protection of cartilage.
