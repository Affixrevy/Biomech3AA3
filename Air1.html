Projectiles with Air Resistance

In the previous lecture ( drag and cycling mechanics ) we saw that drag is the resistance to motion and is a function of the square of velocity.  In this lecture, we will combine the drag coefficient, fluid density and projected area into a single constant called "k".  When an object falls, it has the force of gravity acting downward and the drag force opposing that downward motion.   According to Newton's second law, the sum of these forces is equal to the product of mass and acceleration. Re-balancing this equation, we see that the acceleration of the object is a function of velocity which changes with time.  If the object is dropped with an initial velocity of zero, the acceleration is -g.  However, that acceleration causes an increase in the downward velocity which in turn reduces the acceleration.  When the downward velocity reaches the point when k/m.V2 = g, acceleration will be zero and the velocity will remain constant. This constant velocity is called "terminal velocity" and is equal to the square root of m.g/k.  The value for k is proportional to the cross-sectional area so object with a very high mass to cross-sectional area ratio will be little affected by air resistance whereas objects with a low ratio will be greatly affected.  The famous experiment of Galileo dropping the cannon balls from the leaning tower of Pisa would have turned out quite differently had he used objects that were more influenced by air resistance. 

Without air resistance, the acceleration was a constant -9.81 m/s2 that was easily integrated to yield equations for velocity and displacement.  With air resistance, however, the acceleration function is more difficult to integrate and requires the ability to solve first order differential equations.  In this case, we get a hyperbolic tan function for velocity that reaches a maximum downward value (vt) shown in the lower right graph.  This value is commonly called the terminal velocity and is dependent on mass, k and g as explained in the paragraph above.

If the object starts with a positive or upward velocity, the air resistance will be a downward or negative force since drag always opposes motion.

The velocities and displacements can be calculated with an algorithm similar to the one above by changing the acceleration  formula to have a negative drag term instead of positive.  This new algorithm would apply until the velocity reaches zero at which time the object will be at maximum height and then the original algorithm would apply as the object falls.
