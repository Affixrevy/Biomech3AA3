Introduction to the Advanced Lectures

The way we learn follows stages.  When we first desire to learn about something that we know very little about, we require basic facts that are exactly correct and that we can follow without question.  The first lectures in this biomechanics series presented Newton's laws of motion and the examples and practice problems had exact answers.  These provide a solid base that allow us to progress to the second stage.

The second stage allows for the gray areas of biomechanics to be explored.  We are no longer constrained to the black-and-white problems and we find that there are limits to the rules and that controversy and differences of opinion exist.  We need to have an open mind, find all of the differences of opinion and listen to each opinion or school of thought. The lecture in Biomechanics III and Biomechanics IV will advance our "Black and White" methods but we will also examine the inexact nature of the data that we collect and the procedures that we use.

To begin, let us remember that biomechanics is an inter-disciplinary field of study that blends physics, anatomy, physiology, motor control, neuroscience, mathematics, computer science, material science, engineering, etc. It is not necessary to be an expert in all of these fields to be a successful biomechanist but exposure to each is an asset.  A major theme of the advanced biomechanics lectures is that knowledge of the kinetics of human movement is valuable to the broad spectrum of human movement applications.

Many human movement practitioners (coaches, therapists, prosthetists, etc.) make qualitative observations and proceed to an assessment of what action will improve the situation for their athlete/patient/client.  Since kinetics are the causes of movement, and kinetic variables like force and power cannot be observed qualitatively, these practitioners must make an educated guess that their remedy will address the kinetic cause of the movement disorder.  Quite often, these "educated guesses" are the result of many years of trail-and-error experience and have extremely effective results.  However, it is the bias of these lectures that the ability to determine kinetics is the preferred approach.

Measurement:

The assignment of numbers to observed phenomena (data).  This usually requires some instrumentation and we will be talking about this stage more extensively in the Laboratory portion of these lectures.

Analysis:

This is the process of transforming the measurements into a form that could not be measured directly.  An example would be to take the displacement data from a video data collection session, differentiating twice to get acceleration and multiplying it by mass to get the forces that caused the movement.

Assessment:

This is the decision making step and it is the bias of these lectures that better decisions can be made following a biomechanical analysis that yields kinetics than directly from a qualitative observation based on kinematics.
