Conservation of Energy

Energy can be transfered from potential to kinetic and vice versa. This transfer can occur without any loss of total energy which means that energy is conserved. This conservation of energy occurs when external forces acting on an object do not alter its total energy.

Consider the falling 1.0 kg object below: The object is a projectile under the influence of gravity but if there is no air resistance, the energy is conserved. We will use our projectile equations to determine the kinematics of the falling object at five instants during the first 2 seconds of flight.

Now that we know the kinematics, we can calculate the potential, kinetic and total energy of the 1 kg object at each instant in time. Notice that the total energy is conserved while there is a transfer of potential energy to kinetic energy as the object falls.

Another example of the conservation of energy is a pendulum. We know that as a pendulum swings back and forth, it has zero velocity at the high point and as it swings downward, it gains velocity. This means that as it loses potential energy, it gains kinetic energy. As the pendulum passes the low point at the bottom, it is moving fastest. As it swings upward again, it slows down and its kinetic energy is transfered back to potential energy. If there is no friction or air resistance, the pendulum would swing forever.

In the case when forces alter the energy of an object, work is done and we will look into this in the next lecture.
