Occupational Biomechanics

One of the most important aspects of society is employment and industry.  Biomechanics has much to offer in reducing injury and increasing productivity.  The terms Ergonomics, Human Factors and Occupational Health and Safety have been associated with the use of biomechanics in the work place and I will try to clarify my position on these terms.

Confusion with Ergonomics:

Ergonomics is the study of people at work.  This includes biomechanics but also include psychology, physiology, and numerous other disciplines.  The same can be said for Human Factors and Occupational Health and Safety.  Ergo is Latin for work so Ergomechanics would be a good name for the biomechanics component but the term Occupational Biomechanics is also quite clear. 

History

I do not often include history in my biomechanics lectures but in this case it is important to know how the past is affecting the practice of biomechanics in the work place today and for the future.

 Pre-1900:

Newton's laws of motion had been known for more than 200 years but very little biomechanics was formally being applied to the work place.  Most industry, manufacturing, and farming was done as a craft industry in which the worker was a craftsman and produced the entire product from beginning to end.  Without electricity, people worked during daylight hours and production was relatively slow.  People chose their trade based on convenience without formal training.  They were skilled workers taught at a young age and didn't travel far from home.

Industrial Revolution:

The invention of electricity and the assembly line was a dramatic change in the way people were selected for employment, the types of tasks that were performed and the relationship between the worker and the product.  It was found that production could be increased dramatically by having many workers performing partial steps and working in shifts around the clock.  A worker was only required to perform a single task unlike the craftsman who had to perform the entire production.  Assembly lines were designed to optimize production and workers were hired who could perform the separate tasks.  In this regard, the worker was fit to the task and was discarded if he was injured or otherwise failed to perform at the necessary pace.  Conditions were not controlled by the workers which gave rise to their organizations into trade unions.  Throughout this period, biomechanics was still not formally applied to improve working conditions.  Management tried to increase profits without concern for worker safety and trade unions tried to increase the worker's share of profits and other benefits without actually changing the tasks.

Worker Compensation and Human Rights Legislation:

In the last half of the 20th century, government agencies started compensating injured workers and charging corporations premiums much like an insurance policy.  Companies with few claims had lower premiums than companies with many claims.  It started to become economically important for a company to have a safer work environment.  Additionally, traditional policies of fitting the worker to the task by having minimum height and weight requirements were being challenged as violations of the rights of a job applicant.  The "Americans with Disabilities Act" made it necessary for employers to make reasonable accommodations to tasks in order to not discriminate against individuals with disabilities. The result was a shift to designing assembly lines and occupational tasks that fit the worker rather than the other way around.  Since tasks had to match human requirements and limitations, biomechanics finally was incorporated into industry and the field of Occupational Biomechanics was born.

A good definition of Occupational Biomechanics would be:

The examination of human disorders and performance limitations produced or aggravated by the mismatching of human physical capacities and the performance requirements in industry.

Pre-Employment Screening:

Ideally, every task would be modified to fit every possible worker but this is impossible due to the wide range of human physical capacities and limitations to how much some tasks can be modified.  The employer must often make a compromise between not unjustly denying employment to a capable employee and not predisposing the employee to an unacceptable risk of injury.  As such, a certain degree of pre-employment screening must be done in cases where the physical demands of the job cannot be reduced to a level that fits all people.

The above case illustrates the difficulty employers face and how biomechanists can help. The meat packing company tried to avoid exposing workers to injury by screening out potential employees who might have difficulty performing one of the tasks. However they were found to be in violation which might have been avoided by following these steps:

Step 1: Perform an analysis of the job's physical demands before the screening is done. This allows a more clearly written job description and can lead to a more valid pre-employment screen (see later discussion on validity).  This also allows the employer to inform potential employees about risks and demands of the tasks involved.

Step 2: Identify the most onerous and greatest risk elements of each task and attempt modifications. The biomechanist must keep in mind that any modification must be inexpensive and easily done (satisfies the employer) and is acceptable to workers.  Many modifications are too difficult/expensive to implement or require procedures that the workers will not follow.  Often, workers will not engage safety equipment or move lighter loads more frequently even though they know it is less risky.

Step 3: Carefully design pre-employment screens.  Medical exams that involved x-rays of the lumbar spine were popular in the 1930's but were found to be poorly related with injury.  A medical history of previous low back pain was a good predictor of future problems but potential employees do not faithfully report previous problems when applying for a new job. Fitness level has been found to be a good predictor of injury among fire fighters but being physically fit during a pre-employment screen is not a good indicator of future fitness levels.

The use of linear regression using weight as a predictor of strength shows a positive relationship and it is also known that a given load is less stressful to a stronger individual and presents less risk of injury.  However,  the correlations are low (r=0.3 to r=0.5).  The r2 statistic indicates the explained variance which means that only 9% to 25% of the variance in strength is explained by the variance in weight.  Herrin (1977) used multiple regression of height, weight, and age to explain 36% of the variance in strength but this is still weakly valid and does not prove that smaller, older individuals cannot perform a task or are at increased risk of injury.  The pre-employment task must be as identical as possible to actual tasks and even then, a court could reject it as in the case above.

Step 4: Establish validity of pre-employment screens.  Equal opportunity legislation that requires valid screens does not define validity.  This leaves the validity to be judged by a court during litigation.  However, employers can increase their chances of withstanding court challenges by creating their own database of scores of pre-employment tests, as well as other data from medical exams, questionnaires, and anthropometry in order to establish risk factors.

Since each of these steps involves matching the physical demands of occupational tasks to physical capacities of humans, biomechanists are required to assist employers.
