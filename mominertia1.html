Moment of Inertia

Mass is the amount of matter that an object has and it is also a tenancy to resist linear motion. We call this tenancy to resist motion "inertia". We know from kinematics that an object can have linear and angular motion. The tenancy of a body to resist rotation or angular motion is called the moment of inertia. The moment of inertia is based not only on the amount of mass, but also on the way that mass is distributed relative to the axis of rotation. To illustrate the property of moment of inertia, consider a massless rigid body with an axis at one end and two discrete masses located 0.25m and 0.75m from the axis.

The moment of inertia is calculated as the sum of each mass multiplied by the square of the radial distance of each mass from the axis.

The total mass of this object is 20 kg but the moment of inertia is also influenced by how that mass is distributed about the axis.
