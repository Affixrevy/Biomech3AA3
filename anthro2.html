Anthropometry (Continued)

When we solved problems in static equilibrium, we needed to know how much human limb segments weighed and where that force was acting. For most objects, these data can be determined by weighing the object on a scale and then balancing the object on a knife edge to determine the location of its center. We call this location the "center of mass" or the "center of gravity" and we use "CofG" as the short form in this lecture series.

The above procedure cannot be performed on living human subjects so we use data that was gathered from cadavers. The table below uses the data determined by W.T. Dempster who was a researcher for the US Air Force in the 1950s. He sectioned cadavers into segments and then used a procedure similar to that shown above. The first column gives the names of the segments. The second column defines the proximal and distal ends of each segment. The third column gives the weight of each segment scaled to the whole body weight. The fourth and fifth columns show the location of center of mass of each segment from the proximal and distal ends, respectively. These distances are scaled to the segment lengths which are the distances between the proximal and distal ends. We will talk about radius of gyration later.

Example: Find the forearm mass of a person with a total body mass of 80 kg.

Solution: Look in table and find the forearm segment row, go to the third column and use the "0.016" value.

forearm mass = 0.016 (whole body mass) = 0.016 (80) = 1.28 kg

 

Example: How far is the CofG of the thigh from the hip if the thigh length is 0.38 m.

Solution: Look in the table and find the thigh segment row, go to the fourth column and use the "0.433" value.

The fourth column is used because the hip is the proximal end of the thigh segment.

thigh CofG = 0.433 (thigh length) = 0.433 (0.38) = 0.1645 m.

 

Example: How far is the CofG of the Forearm and Hand segment from the elbow if the distance between the elbow and wrist is 0.28m?

Solution: Even though the segment includes the hand, the fourth column of the table has a value of "0.682" for the distance of the CofG from the proximal end (elbow). Therefore, the CofG of the forearm and hand segment is 0.28(0.682) = 0.191 m from the elbow.
