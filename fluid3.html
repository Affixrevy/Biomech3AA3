Fluid Resistance

Lift: The component of air resistance that acts at right angles to the direction of the approaching air.

Drag: The component of air resistance that acts in the direction of the approaching air.

The angle of attack (θ) affects the amount of lift and drag. As the angle increases from zero to 90 degrees, drag increases from a minimum to a maximum. Although lift and drag are orthogonal, lift is not a maximum when drag is a minimum. In order to create lift, the angle of attack must be greater than zero which means that there will also be some drag. The lift force can be directed downward with a negative angle of attack.

Surface Drag: 

Surface drag is the resistance derived from friction between the surface of a body and the fluid through which it is moving. The fluid particles adjacent to the body slow down, causing turbulent flow. The magnitude of surface drag depends on a number of factors; it increases with the relative velocity of fluid flow, the surface area over which the flow occurs; the roughness of the body surface; and the viscosity of the fluid.

 

Form Drag:

Often called "pressure drag" or "wake drag" it is more significant than surface drag for objects that do not have a streamlined shape. The streamlined shape of a football has only two thirds the drag of a spherical ball of the same cross-sectional area. As an object moves through the fluid or air, the fluid separates and flows past the object. This is called boundary layer flow and can be either laminar or turbulent. At low velocity, the laminar flow creates a low pressure area behind the object which tends to pull the object backward into the low pressure area.  This backward pull is the form or pressure drag. As the velocity of the object increases, the boundary layer flow transitions from laminar to turbulent.  The velocity at which that transition occurs is called the critical Reynolds number. The Reynolds number is a unit less number which is the product of the ball diameter and velocity divided by the kinematic viscosity of the air. The Reynolds number of a baseball traveling at 20 m/s is about 100,000 and the critical value is about 150,000 which means as the velocity of the ball exceeds 30 m/s, the boundary flow transitions from laminar to turbulent and the drag coefficient decreases. Due to its small diameter, the golf ball has a smaller Reynolds number than the baseball and it reaches its critical value of 40,000 at about 25 m/s. Notice that the dimples keep the drag coefficient at about 0.25 for a range of Reynolds numbers from 60,000 to over 200,000 which corresponds to a range of velocities from 30 to over 100 m/s.

It is important to note that even though the coefficient of drag CD decreases above the critical Reynolds number, the drag force is a function of the velocity squared (see the "Drag and Cycling Mechanics" lecture) which means that if we plot the drag force over a range of velocities that span the laminar and turbulent flow conditions, the drag force still increases with velocity just a lower rate than if the flow remained as laminar.
