Moment of Force / (Torque)

Examples of changing moment arm lengths:

Since the torque or moment is determined by both the magnitude of the force and the length of the moment arm, you can change the effectiveness of the force by changing the moment arm length. This happens naturally within the human body as shown in the figure below. As the elbow flexes from full extension to about 90 degrees, the moment arm of the biceps muscle increases which means that even if the biceps force does not change, the amount of torque that it generates increases. As the elbow continues to flex past 90 degrees, the moment arm decreases. This changing moment arm is an example of gaining a mechanical advantage by means of using leverage. Levers will be discussed in greater detail in the next lecture.

Alternate Method of Calculating Moments

Quite often, a second method of calculating moments is preferred. If the location of the axis and the point of application of the force is given in X and Y coordinates and/or there are more than one force acting, this alternate method may be a better way to find the moment of force.

In the example to the left we are tasked with finding the moment of force about the shoulder axis given the coordinates of (0,0) for the point of application of Serratus Anterior, coordinates of (0.05, 0.13) m for the axis of rotation and a muscle force of 400 N. Our first method to find the moment of force would be to find the moment arm, multiply by 400N and then use the right hand rule to get the direction (positive or negative).

This alternate approach resolves the force into two rectangular components and uses the X and Y dimensions for moment arms and then adds the two moments. The X component of force would be 400cos20o = 376 N and its moment would be 376(0.13) = 48.9 N.m. The Y component of force would be 400sin20o = 136.8 N and its moment would be 136.8(0.05) = 6.84 N.m. The moment of force would then be 48.9 + 6.84 = 55.7 N.m. We still must use the right hand rule to see if either of the two components is a negative moment before we calculate the sum.

As stated above, this approach has some advantages but I still believe that the ability to visualize moment arms has tremendous value in biomechanics because it allows us to make qualitative assessments of situations and how to improve them if we wish to maximize torque for performance or minimize torque for injury prevention.
