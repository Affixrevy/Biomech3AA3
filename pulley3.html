Wedges

A wedge is also considered to be a simple machine. It can be very useful to lift heavy objects a short distance. Friction is a key component to prevent the wedge from slipping out from underneath the heavy object after the force is no longer applied.

As an example, consider the heavy block shown to the right. A wedge can be used to lift the 1000 N block by applying force P to the wedge. In order to determine the amount of force required, we must consider the friction forces as well as gravity. Assume the coefficient of friction between all contacting surfaces is 0.2 and the mass of the wedge is negligible.

Our first step is to draw a free body diagram of the 1000 N block. We include the force of gravity (1000 N), the friction force opposing the block sliding up the wall(F1), the normal force of the wall acting on the block (N1), the friction force opposing the wedge sliding along the bottom of the block (F2), and the normal force of the wedge acting on the block (N2).

We can see that we have four unknowns and if we try to solve this static equilibrium problem, we will only have two equations. We can reduce our unknowns by using the friction formula (F = μ.N) which means the F1 = 0.2N1 and F2 = 0.2N2.

We can now solve for the unknown forces by taking the sum of the horizontal and vertical components to be zero. We use equation [1] substituting for F1 to get N1 as a function of N2. We then substitute for F1 and N1 in equation [2] to find the normal force acting on the block by the wedge needs to be 1214 N in order to overcome gravity and friction to start motion of the block.

In order to find P, we must also draw a free body diagram of the wedge.

The wedge will have the reaction force of the block (F2 and N2) acting in the opposite directions (Newton's third law), the applied force (P), the friction force of the floor opposing the sliding of the wedge (F3) and the normal force of the floor acting on the wedge (N3).

Again, we write the two static equilibrium equations and substitute the known values for N2 and F2 to solve for N3 and then P. Notice that P = 796 N is less than the 1000 N gravitational force acting on the block. This means that the wedge has offered a force advantage even though it must also overcome friction in order to cause the block to move.

If we used a heavy hammer to deliver blows that had forces in excess of 796 N, the block would be lifted. What happens when we stop hammering? Why does the wedge not slip back out when P = 0?

In order to answer that question, we would re-draw our free body diagrams without P and since the impending motion due to gravity would be opposite, we would reverse the direction of the three frictional forces. F1 would oppose the block sliding downward and even though N2 would still be tending to push the wedge out, F2 and F3 will be more than sufficient to keep the wedge in place. This is mostly due to the slight angle (15o) and the coefficient of friction (μ = 0.2). Without friction, the wedge could not remain supporting the block and if the angle is too steep for a given coefficient of friction, the wedge will also slip back out.
