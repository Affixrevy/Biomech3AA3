Teaching Philosophy

What is Biomechanics: I have found it necessary to have two definitions of the biomechanics of human motion. One definition is an operational definition that clearly defines the field of study and serves as a guide to those of us studying and working in the field and the other definition is a less technical definition that is more easily understood by the lay person.

Operational Definition Biomechanics of human motion is the study of the effects and control of forces that act on and are produced by the human body. The central word is "forces" which are the fundamental causes of motion and reminds us that we need to be concerned with kinetics (causes) as well as kinematics (descriptions). The forces that "act on" the human body are external forces such as gravity and the reactions of the ground and other objects. Forces that are "produced by" the human body are internal forces such as muscle, ligament and joint forces. The "control" aspect to the definition reminds us that we need to be concerned with neuromuscular control.

Lay Definition Biomechanics of human motion is the application of the laws of physics to improve the safety and performance of workers, athletes, and clinical populations such as amputees, children with cerebral palsy, and many others. This definition is more vague but it is better understood by lay people and the inclusion of some common applications emphasizes its importance to those who would not otherwise make the association.

From the definition of biomechanics, it follows that one needs to have a solid understanding of the mechanics of human motion. The level of understanding is reflected by the ability to solve a vast array of applied problems. Solutions to applied problems require tools such as math, physics, and engineering techniques because the solutions are often contrary to our common sense. Common sense might cause one to think that if I can perform a single somersault from a 2 meter platform, I should be able to perform a double somersault from a four meter platform because I will be in the air for twice as long. However, those who know the physics of projectiles and the law of gravity know that height is proportional to the square of time and that I really need a height that is four times larger to allow me twice as long in the air. How is it possible that we can live our entire lives in an environment that is controlled by physical laws and yet most of us have a very poor understanding of those laws and their consequences? I think that at least part of the answer has to do with the fact that visually, we perceive displacement and velocity but not acceleration. Acceleration is proportional to the forces causing motion but the kinematics that we perceive (displacement and velocity) are nonlinearly related to the kinetics that cause motion and we are not very good at thinking nonlinearly (most of us, anyway). In addition to being contrary to common sense, the first step to solving an applied problem is usually the most difficult step. The result, like much of mathematics is a frustrating feeling that problems seem impossible to solve until a light is mysteriously turned on and then the solutions seem elementary.

How is the light "turned on"?

Most math, physics and engineering textbooks provide the theory of a certain topic followed by an example of a solved problem and then provide several "exercise problems". The answers to the exercise problems are usually but not always given. It is implied that, although frustrating, mastery of the topic is best achieved by struggling with the exercise problems until the light is "turned on". It is my opinion that this has resulted in a dichotomy in our society between a minority who can do math and physics, and the majority who cannot. This frustration causes students to withdraw from math oriented studies and to remain under confident and unskilled in applied problem solving. Since solutions to these sorts of problems (and understanding) often requires overcoming common sense and since hard work is not a guarantee that the light will be "turned on", there is a belief by many in our society that you must be born with an aptitude for math and physics in order to be successful. Those with this belief use it as an excuse for their lack of skill in these areas and some even go so far as to claim that boys are better at math than girls and that certain ethnic groups have an advantage or disadvantage. Research has failed to find any genetic or biological cause and the reasons for any difference can only be considered to be cultural at this time. Click here for more information on the lack of women and various ethnic groups in the fields of mathematics, science, and engineering. The bottom line is that whatever differences may exist between genders or cultures, I believe those differences can be eliminated with proper attitudes and training. I hope that these lectures provide the proper training to students with the proper attitude. For the student, it is therefore important to believe that any problem can be solved and that there are no barriers of any kind.

It is clear to me that a better method of learning applied problem solving and, as a consequence, gaining a better understanding of math, science, and engineering is needed. The method needs to be delivered in a way that overcomes gender, racial and other cultural barriers and needs to have a stronger relationship between hard work and success and have less frustration getting started. I have developed a method of teaching called Smart Tutor™ with these goals in mind and it is my hope that this will allow the light to be "turned on" for the majority of our society rather than the minority.
