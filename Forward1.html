Forward Dynamics and Human Movement Simulation

The lecture on dynamic equilibrium used Newton's second law and the inverse dynamics approach to solve the internal kinetics (joint reaction forces and net joint moments).  Inverse dynamics is used when masses (inertia) and accelerations (kinematics) are known and forces (kinetics) are calculated. The forward dynamics approach is used when the kinetics and inertia are known and the kinematics are calculated. The diagram at the right shows a single segment controlled by a moment of force or torque that tends to rotate the segment about the proximal end (T1).

The angular acceleration can be calculated by dividing the net joint moment (T1) by the moment of inertia.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Once the angular acceleration is found, the angular velocity and angular displacement can be determined via integration. This integration is performed by the computer in a way that is similar to the method that we used to find the velocity and displacement of an object falling with air resistance. Namely, the acceleration is considered to have occurred for a very short period of time and the area or velocity is determined by multiplying the acceleration by the time interval. The displacement is found by considering that velocity occurred for the same short time period and multiplying the velocity by that time. The computer iteratively calculates accelerations, velocities and displacements for many small time intervals over the period of the simulation.  

A system of two segments controlled by T1 and T2 is more complicated.  The angular acceleration of segment 1 is effected by more than just T1.  There are two equations which must be solved to yield the two angular accelerations.

The angular accelerations of the two segments are affected by the angular velocity of segment 2 due to coriolis effects which is affected by the relative joint angle as well as the masses, lengths and moments of inertia.  The example below shows a very simple situation of a system at rest and only a moment generated about joint 2.

It can be seen that even though T1 = 0, both segments accelerate due to the interactions between the two segments. T2 causes a positive acceleration of segment 2 which is expected but it also caused a negative acceleration of segment 1.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

The accelerations above will cause changes in the segment velocities and the joint angles which will cause the accelerations to change even if T1 and T2 remain 0 N.m and 4 N.m, respectively. Computer simulations perform the integrations of these accelerations over a very short time period to obtain the changes in velocity and displacement and then re-calculate the new accelerations in an iterative process that simulates the movement outcomes of the joint moments (T1 and T2).

The table on the left is the output from a computer simulation of the above example that T2 = 4 N.m for 0.1 seconds and then both moments are zero.  It can be seen that the velocities (Omega2 and Omega1) change and so too do the joint angles (Theta2 and Theta1).  Also notice that the segments continue to accelerate even after the moments are turned off (after 0.1 seconds).  

A three segment system becomes even more involved with the equations becoming much longer and the need to solve the three unknowns simultaneously (see below).  Computer methods allow the solution to be found and enable the biomechanics researcher to create fairly complex multi-segment models of the human body and calculate the resulting accelerations from known joint torques.

It is even possible to replace the joint torques with individual muscle forces for an even higher degree of similarity to human movement and conduct experiments on the best way to perform a skill.  Such experiments have used the term "in-silico" to separate an experiment performed by computer simulation from other biological terms such as "in-vivo", "in-vitro" and in-situ".

Levels of understanding:

1) "Don't know what happened" - no observation, description or empirical evidence

2) "Know what happened but don't know why it happened" - kinematic description but know kinetic causes

3) "Know the causes but don't know how to change the result" - kinetic analysis but unsure of the consequences of making a change

4) "Know what happened, why it happened and how to change the outcome".

Pros and Cons of Human Simulations:

Pros    -perfectly controlled experiment (A causes B)

            -no experimental error due to transducers or noise

            - inexpensive equipment (computer)

            - test hypotheses that are experimentally unethical on human or animal subjects

Cons    - mathematically complex

            - validity of results limited to accuracy of model to real human subjects

            - few clinical applications.

In-Silico Experiments:

- role of two joint muscles in the vertical jump

- most economical method of walking

- surgical alteration consequences

- technique for reduced ACL strain.
