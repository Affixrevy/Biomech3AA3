Total Work and Energy Transfers Between Segments

Total work on the lower extremity:

On the previous page, we calculated the work done on a thigh segment over a time period during a walking stride.  If we wanted to calculate the total work done on the entire lower extremity, we would calculate the work done on each segment by calculating the change in each segment's energy and then add them together.

The total work performed is 3.8 J.  Notice that the thigh segment lost 1.7 J of energy while the leg gained 1.5 J and the foot gained 4.0 J.  This means that 1.7 J of energy was transfered from the thigh to the leg and the foot.  an additional 3.8 J of work was needed to move the lower extremity.  On the previous page, we saw that energy can be conserved by transfers within a segment and now we see that energy can be transfered between segments.

Caution should be used with this formula when the segments include the whole body or when a limb segment is losing energy but not able to transfer the energy to a segment gaining energy.  An example would be a person lowering one arm at the same time as they are raising the other arm.  The formula above would yield a value of zero work yet in reality, negative work is being done by the muscles lowering one arm and positive work is being done by muscle raising the other arm.  For this reason, many biomechanists do not allow transfers between segments through the trunk unless the body is airborne.
