Basic Electricity for Biomechanics

Definitions:

Electricity - the force created by an imbalance in the number of electrons at two points.

Electrons - negatively charged particles.

Current - the movement of electrons from higher potential to lower potential

Ampere - the unit of current or rate of electron movevment

Voltage - electromotive force created by the difference in electron population between two points.

Resistance - opposition to electron flow measured in ohms.

Watts - units of electrical power = volts x amps

Capacitance - the ability of a material to store electricity.

Ohm's Law:

The current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference (Voltage). The constant of this proportionality is the resistance. This proportionality was discovered by German physicist Georg Ohm in 1827 who followed the work of Alessandro Volta who invented the first battery. Volta's work extended Luigi Galvani's work on electricity generated by animal bodies to more general use of chemistry to generate electricity.

Electrical Safety:

Damage to biological tissue is due to excessive current. We know that current flows from a higher to lower potential and the higher the resistance, for a given voltage, the slower the electrons flow and the lower the current. Therefore, we would like to keep voltage low and resistance high in order to protect against tissue damage. Individual body chemisrty has a significant impact on how electric current affects different individuals. The use of rubber gloves and other poor conductors increases the resistance and decreases the current of a given voltage. Water and even sweat decreases resistance considerably.

The alternating current from an electrical socket is 120 V in Canada and wires are usually covered with rubber or plastic insulation.  Since current seeks the lowest potential, a ground wire (three pronged plug) allows a path that helps to prevent the human body becoming the pathway.  It is also more dangerous when the current passes through the chest from one side of the body to the other because 500 milliamps of current can cause heart fibrillation. For this reason, it is a good practice to use only one hand when working on live circuits of hazardous voltage .
