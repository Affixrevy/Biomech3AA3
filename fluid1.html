Fluid Mechanics

The following lecture introduces some of the basic concepts of fluid mechanics that pertain to the biomechanics of human motion.

Buoyant Force: The resultant of all vertical forces exerted on a body by the fluid that it is immersed in.

 

Depending on how density is distributed within the body, the center of buoyancy can have a different location than the center of gravity. Since the gravitational and buoyant forces are the only forces acting on a floating object, the object will orient itself such that the two forces have equal and opposite directions

 

 

 

The magnitude of the buoyant force is equal to the weight of the fluid displaced (Achimedes Principle).

 

Density = mass / volume

If an object has a density that is greater than the density of water, it will not float. It can only displace enough water that equals its own volume and since the weight of the water will not be equal to its own weight, the gravitational force will be greater than the buoyant force.

Example: How much mass can be added to a canoe that has a mass of 50kg and a volume of 400 liters before it will sink in fresh water?

Solution: The canoe has a density of 50/400 = 0.125 kg/liter which is much less than water and it will float. In fact, it will only displace 50 liters of water to achieve a buoyant force equal to its weight. As mass is added to the canoe, the gravitational force pushes it lower in the water until the buoyant force is equal to the gravitational force. Mass can be added to the canoe until it displaces its entire 400 liters of water which means that 350 kg of mass can be added to the 50 kg mass of the canoe to equal 400 kg of displaced water.

If we consider the human body to be composed of muscle, fat, and lean tissue, we must also consider the densities of each type of tissue. Fat is less dense than water but muscle (lean tissue) and bone have greater densities than water. A person's ability to float has nothing to do with their swimming ability but is determined by their overall density. As infants, humans have a large percentage of fat and float quite easily. As we age to be young adults, the percentage of fat decreases and muscle increases which causes an increase in density. Older adults have less muscle and their bones have lost density as well which increases their ability to float.
