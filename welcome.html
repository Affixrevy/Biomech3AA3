What is Biomechanics? 

I have found it necessary to have two definitions of the biomechanics of human motion. One definition is an operational definition that clearly defines the field of study and serves as a guide to those of us studying and working in the field and the other definition is a less technical definition that is more easily understood by the lay person.

Operational Definition Biomechanics of human motion is the study of the effects and control of forces that act on and are produced by the human body. The central word is "forces" which are the fundamental causes of motion and reminds us that we need to be concerned with kinetics (causes) as well as kinematics (descriptions). The forces that "act on" the human body are external forces such as gravity and the reactions of the ground and other objects. Forces that are "produced by" the human body are internal forces such as muscle, ligament and joint forces. The "control" aspect to the definition reminds us that we need to be concerned with neuromuscular control.

Lay Definition Biomechanics of human motion is the application of the laws of physics to improve the safety and performance of workers, athletes, and clinical populations such as amputees, children with cerebral palsy, and many others. This definition is more vague but it is better understood by lay people and the inclusion of some common applications emphasizes its importance to those who would not otherwise make the association.

What is Mechanics? 

Before starting our study of biomechanics of human motion, let's first examine the field of mechanics.  Mechanics is a branch of physics that studies bodies at rest and in motion.  The study of bodies at rest is called "Statics" and the study of bodies in motion is called "Dynamics".  Many of the principles and laws of motion are more easily understood in the static situation and we will learn these before moving on to dynamics.  Two other terms that are very important to know as a student of mechanics are kinematics and kinetics.  Kinematic variables describe motion and include displacement, velocity and acceleration, while kinetic variables cause motion and include force, work, energy, power, momentum, etc.  The reason these terms are so important to the biomechanist is that we use kinematics as a vehicle to get to kinetics.  Kinematics can be important to answer many questions in biomechanics but it is the kinetics that cause things to happen and are most important to understand.  As with statics and dynamics, we will learn kinematics before moving on to kinetics.  The diagram below shows this relationship more clearly.
